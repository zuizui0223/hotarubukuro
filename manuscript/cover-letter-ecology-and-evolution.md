Dear Editors,

Please consider our manuscript, “Flower-colour state and conditional visible
intensity in *Campanula punctata*: environmental and spatial structure across
Japan,” as a Research Article in *Ecology and Evolution*.

Geographical studies of intraspecific floral colour are constrained by sparse
quantitative trait data and by the tendency to combine pigment absence,
variation among pigmented flowers, broad spatial structure, and local biotic
hypotheses in one response or model. We converted 1,965 author-reviewed,
route-linked YAMAP records into a supervised optical phenotype and analysed
flower-colour state separately from visible intensity conditional on
pigmentation. Geographically blocked INLA-SPDE models then defined the national
environmental and continuous spatial baseline.

Temperature was conditionally associated with both response parts, whereas
soil and topographic axes differed between colour state and pigmented-only
intensity. A predicted five-species *Bombus* community fingerprint added little
national discrimination but corresponded to local turnover in both colour
components relative to 1,000 replicated natural maps. Locally isolated
pigmented observations were not excessive under the same baseline, and
human-context contrasts did not pass familywise correction. These bounded
positive and negative results show how non-random, georeferenced community
photographs can support scale-specific ecological inference without treating
predicted suitability as pollinator abundance or exploratory human context as
horticultural provenance.

We believe the manuscript fits *Ecology and Evolution* because it combines a
general problem in trait biogeography with an explicit evidence hierarchy:
supervised digital phenotyping, national environmental and spatial structure,
a pre-specified local biotic test, and a predictive reference check for
putative human-associated departures. The workflow and its limitations are
transferable to other conspicuous traits represented in route-linked image
collections.
